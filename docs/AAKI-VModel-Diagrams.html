1  The AAKI pipeline — from documents to a governed knowledge graph

The three stages turn source knowledge into a system of record. The AI assistant collaborates at every stage but never owns a decision — outputs land in a Propose state for a named human to approve. Inputs that define meaning (left) are deliberately separated from inputs that populate instances (right of the server).

The server is the system of record; the catalog template makes it interoperable with other OSLC tools out of the box.

3  AAKI mapped onto the V-model — the traceability graph

In the V-model every left-side artifact carries a verification obligation to a right-side artifact. In OSLC terms each ↔ is a typed link (oslc_rm:validatedBy, oslc_qm:validatesRequirement…). The V is not a document — it is a live link graph. AAKI's three stages define what valid traceability looks like, instantiate the artifacts and links, and activate the graph for decisions.

4  Three layers of AI assistance across the tool chain

An AI assistant connected by MCP sees three scopes. Tool-local authoring, cross-tool integration over the OSLC link graph, and enterprise analytics over a materialized view (LQE). The feedback loop runs top-to-bottom and back.

5  Governing the assistant: skills, rules & agents per ASPICE

The workspace ships three Claude skills (one per AAKI stage) plus governance rules that keep the AI a collaborator, not an agent on the RACI chart. Authority is graded Observe → Propose → Execute, and every AI action is attributed to a named human via provenance — which is exactly what an ASPICE / ISO 26262 audit needs to see.
